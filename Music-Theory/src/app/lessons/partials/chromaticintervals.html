Chromatic Intervals

Diatonic intervals are often not descriptive enough when it comes to understanding the exact nature of the interval, so in most cases it is much more useful to use chromatic intervals

Chromatic intervals are measured in whole steps and half steps as well as note letters away. The number of note letters away give the interval the number name. For example, in the interval C to Eb, E is three note letters away from C, so it will be a some kind of chromatic interval of a third.

Depending on the quality, the chromatic interval will be prefixed with either major, minor, diminished, augmented, or perfect.

Chromatic intervals with the same notes can have different names depending on the note names you give them. For example, even though F# and Gb are the same note, C to F# would be an augmented fourth because F is four note letters away from C, but C to Gb would be a diminished fifth because G is five note letters away from C.

In practice people generally will remember the interval by the sound of it, rather then the number of steps.

So how do you know what prefix you should give the interval?

The prefix of major, minor, augmented, diminished, or perfect is given to the interval to describe the interval's quality, i.e. does the interval sound major, minor, augmented, diminished, or perfect. Major and augmented intervals are larger than minor and diminished intervals. A flatted major interval will be minor, and a flatted perfect or minor interval will be diminished. A sharpened minor interval will be major, and a sharpened major or perfect interval will be augmented.

A tritone is an interval that splits the octave exactly in half. Since there are 12 half steps or 6 whole steps in an octave, the tritone would be the interval that is 6 half steps or 3 whole steps from the original note.

To internalize the sound of these intervals, I recommend trying to sing the the first two notes of any melody that you know and figure out what interval it is.
