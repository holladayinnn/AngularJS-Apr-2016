Whole Steps and Half Steps

Now that you know the names of the notes, you need to understand how to relate the notes to each other.

One way to do so is to use whole steps and half steps, also known as wholetones and semitones respectively.

A half step is the smallest distance in traditonal western music. It literally means one note away. Some examples of half-steps are E to F, C to C#, or (going down) G to Gb.

A whole step means two notes away and is equal to two half steps. Some examples of whole-steps are E to F#, C to D, or (going down) G to F.

Whole steps and half steps are the basic building blocks of scales, intervals, melodies, and chords.

Note: I added one extra key to the piano this time (an extra C on top). Notice that there are no sharps and flats (or black notes) between B and C and between E and F. This means, a half step up from E is F. F is actually synonymous with E#, and E is synonymous with Fb. Similarly, a half step up from B is C. Therefore, C is synonymous with B#, and B is synonymous with Cb.

Also Note: Notes with the same name can actually have different frequencies. The two C's on the piano below are both called C, but one sounds higher than the other. The C that is higher is actually exactly double the frequency of the C that is lower. The relationship between these two C's is called an octave.
